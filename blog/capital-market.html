Introduction

The capital market plays a crucial role in the modern economy by facilitating the flow of funds between investors and businesses. It's important to understand the distinction between key terms:

  • Capital: Money that an owner invests in their own business
  • Investment: Not the same as capital. Investment refers to outsiders' money in a company

A capital market is where instruments like bonds, debentures, equity, or securities are traded. The primary role of the capital market is to channel investments from investors who have surplus funds to those who are running a deficit or need funding.

Example: If XYZ company requires 10 lakh rupees to expand its infrastructure, they might issue company shares or bonds to the market to collect funds from the public who have surplus funds and are willing to be part of XYZ company.

Meaning and Definition

The capital market is a market where buyers and sellers engage in the trade of financial securities like bonds, stocks, etc. The buying and selling are undertaken by participants such as individuals and institutions. Generally, this market trades mostly in long-term securities (bonds, stocks, equity shares, preference shares).

The primary objective of the capital market is to raise long-term funds for companies (it does not deal with short-term financing).

Example: If you have a surplus fund of 50,000 rupees and you keep it in your locker, you'll only have the same amount at the end of the year (which might be affected by the time value of money). However, if you invest that amount in the capital market, it could generate interest or dividends for you. This opportunity is provided by the capital market.

Characteristics of Capital Market

Key Features

  • Deals in long-term securities
  • Has two main segments: Equity and Debentures
  • Performs trade-off function
  • Helps in capital formation
  • Creates liquidity in the market

Two Main Segments:

  1. Equity - Represents real ownership of the company through shareholding. Equity shareholders:
    • Don't receive fixed dividends
    • Receive dividends based on company profits, as decided in board meetings
    • Are the last to receive repayment when a company closes
    • Only receive dividends if the company makes a profit
    • May have to pay the company if it makes a loss
  2. Debentures - Held by third-party members of the organization or company. Debenture holders:
    • Are not owners and have no voting rights
    • Receive their investment money at the end of the maturity period
    • Receive a fixed interest amount every year (predetermined)

Liquidity: The ability that determines how fast a company can convert its assets to money. In this market, both selling and buying activities exist. In the primary market, companies release their shares into the market, while in the secondary market, those shares are traded.

Scope/Importance of Capital Market

  • Mobilization of savings - The capital market creates a habit of saving among market participants. Without this market, people might keep their money themselves or spend it on goods or services for short-term satisfaction, without getting any profit or value addition.
  • Long-term funds - Provides a platform for raising long-term capital for businesses and governments, enabling them to finance large-scale projects and expansions.
  • Capital formation - Helps in the formation of capital by channeling savings into investments, which is crucial for economic development.
  • Proper regulation of funds - Ensures that funds are allocated efficiently and according to regulations, preventing misuse and ensuring optimal utilization.
  • Speeds up economic growth - By facilitating investment in productive sectors, it accelerates economic development and creates employment opportunities.

Functions/Roles of Capital Market

Primary Functions

  • Capital formation
  • Economic growth
  • Industrial growth
  • Technical assistance
  • Regulation of funds
  • Mobilization of savings
  • Connects investors and institutions
  • Availability of funds

The capital market serves as a crucial intermediary in the financial system, performing several vital functions:

  • Capital formation - Aggregates small savings into productive capital, enabling large-scale investments that would otherwise be impossible.
  • Economic growth - Facilitates investment in productive sectors, accelerating economic development by ensuring efficient allocation of resources.
  • Industrial growth - Provides necessary funding for industrial expansion and development, fostering innovation and technological advancement.
  • Technical assistance - Offers expertise and guidance for efficient fund utilization, helping businesses make informed investment decisions.
  • Regulation of funds - Ensures proper allocation and utilization of financial resources through regulatory frameworks and oversight.
  • Mobilization of savings - Encourages saving habits among the public by offering investment opportunities with potential returns.
  • Connects investors and institutions - Bridges the gap between those with surplus funds and those who need capital, creating a mutually beneficial ecosystem.
  • Availability of funds - Ensures a steady supply of capital for various economic activities, preventing liquidity crunches.

Types of Capital Market

Under financial institutions, we have two main categories:

Market Structure

  • Capital Market (Primary and Secondary)
  • Money Market
  1. Capital Market
    • Primary Market - Specifically for IPOs (Initial Public Offerings) where shares and other securities are issued for the first time. This is where companies directly raise capital from investors.
    • Secondary Market - Where already existing securities are traded between investors. The company doesn't receive additional funds from these transactions, but it creates liquidity for investors.
  2. Money Market - Deals with short-term financial instruments with maturities typically less than one year. It serves different purposes than the capital market, focusing on short-term liquidity needs.

Capital Market Instruments

Capital market instruments are financial tools that are used to raise capital. These include:

Key Instruments

  • Equity Shares
  • Preference Shares
  • Debentures
  • Bonds
  • Commercial Papers
  • Certificate of Deposits
  • Equity Shares - Represent ownership in a company and give shareholders voting rights and dividends based on company performance.
  • Preference Shares - Have priority over equity shares in dividend payments and asset distribution during liquidation, but typically don't carry voting rights.
  • Debentures - Long-term debt instruments issued by companies that pay fixed interest and must be repaid at maturity.
  • Bonds - Debt securities issued by governments or corporations that pay periodic interest and return principal at maturity.
  • Commercial Papers - Short-term unsecured promissory notes issued by corporations to meet short-term liabilities.
  • Certificate of Deposits - Short-term deposit instruments issued by banks that can be traded in the secondary market.

Structure of Capital Market

The capital market structure consists of various components that work together to facilitate the flow of funds:

Key Components

  • Primary Market
  • Secondary Market
  • Financial Intermediaries
  • Regulatory Bodies
  • Investors
  • Primary Market - Where new securities are issued and companies raise fresh capital directly from investors.
  • Secondary Market - Where existing securities are traded among investors, providing liquidity to the market.
  • Financial Intermediaries - Banks, insurance companies, mutual funds, and other institutions that facilitate transactions between issuers and investors.
  • Regulatory Bodies - Securities and Exchange Board, Central Bank, and other authorities that oversee market operations and ensure compliance with regulations.
  • Investors - Individual and institutional investors who provide capital in exchange for financial returns.

Difference Between Capital and Money Market

While both capital and money markets are important components of the financial system, they serve different purposes and have distinct characteristics:

Basis Capital Market Money Market
Time Period Long-term (more than 1 year) Short-term (less than 1 year)
Instruments Shares, bonds, debentures Treasury bills, commercial papers, certificates of deposit
Risk Higher risk Lower risk
Return Higher return potential Lower return potential
Liquidity Less liquid More liquid
Purpose Capital formation and investment Working capital and liquidity management
Participants Retail investors, institutional investors, corporations Banks, financial institutions, corporations
Regulation More regulated Less regulated

Global Capital Market

The global capital market refers to the worldwide network where international investors trade securities and companies raise capital across national boundaries. As economies become increasingly interconnected, the global capital market plays a crucial role in facilitating international investment and economic growth.

Key Features

  • International Integration
  • Cross-border Investments
  • Foreign Exchange Markets
  • International Financial Institutions
  • Global Regulatory Framework
  • International Integration - Markets across countries are interconnected, allowing capital to flow freely across borders in search of the best investment opportunities.
  • Cross-border Investments - Capital flows between different countries, enabling investors to diversify their portfolios globally and companies to access international funding sources.
  • Foreign Exchange Markets - Currency exchange is an integral part of the global capital market, facilitating international transactions and investments.
  • International Financial Institutions - Organizations like the World Bank, IMF, and regional development banks play important roles in the global capital market, providing funding, technical assistance, and regulatory oversight.
  • Global Regulatory Framework - International standards and regulations govern operations to ensure stability, transparency, and investor protection across different jurisdictions.

Conclusion

The capital market serves as a vital component of any economy by facilitating the efficient allocation of resources. It provides a platform for businesses to raise long-term capital and for investors to grow their wealth. Understanding the structure, functions, and instruments of the capital market is essential for anyone looking to participate in it effectively.

Key Takeaways

  • Capital markets facilitate the flow of funds from savers to borrowers
  • They play a crucial role in economic development and capital formation
  • Primary and secondary markets serve different but complementary functions
  • Various instruments like equity, bonds, and debentures offer different risk-return profiles
  • Global integration has expanded investment opportunities across borders

As global economies become increasingly interconnected, the importance of well-functioning capital markets continues to grow. They not only support economic growth but also contribute to financial stability and prosperity. By channeling savings into productive investments, capital markets help create jobs, foster innovation, and improve living standards.

For investors, understanding the nuances of capital markets can lead to better investment decisions and portfolio management. For businesses, these markets provide essential funding for growth, expansion, and innovation. And for economies as a whole, robust capital markets are indicators of financial health and development.
